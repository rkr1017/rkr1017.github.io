Experiment Design

Metric Choice

 

Invariant metrics chosen:

Number of cookies: Number of unique cookies to view the course overview page

Number of clicks: Number of unique cookies to click the “Start free trial” button

 

Both experiment and control groups visit the page initially, so “Number of cookies” is invariant and both groups see two buttons initially, so “Number of clicks” should not be different (is invariant).

 

Evaluation metrics chosen:

Gross conversion: Number of user-ids to complete checkout and enroll in the free trial divided by number of unique cookies to click the "Start free trial" button

Retention:  Number of user-ids to remain enrolled past the 14-day boundary (and thus make at least one payment) divided by number of user-ids to complete checkout.

Net conversion: Number of user-ids to remain enrolled past the 14-day boundary (and thus make at least one payment) divided by the number of unique cookies to click the "Start free trial" button.

 

“Gross conversion” represents those who made a decision to enroll after passing commitment time test, which should be different in an experimental group compared to control.

“Retention” means user moved further down the road and made a payment.

“Net conversion” tracks users clicked "Start free trial" button and made a payment.

All three metrics track activities unique to the experimental group.

 

“Gross conversion” tracks users who started trial, stayed on course and enrolled. That metric is evaluating the first part of the hypothesis “…this might set clearer expectations for students upfront, thus reducing the number of frustrated students who left the free trial because they didn't have enough time”. Would those who started be less likely to leave before enrolling? It is expected to have a higher value in an experiment group.

“Net conversion” tracks a group of users, those who stayed for at least 14 days after starting a trial. It is evaluating the second part of the initial hypothesis: “without significantly reducing the number of students to continue past the free trial and eventually complete the course“. Would those who started trial stay for the most part? It is expected to be not much different between control and experiment.

“Retention” tracks a group of users, those who stayed for at least 14 days after enrolling. Would those who enrolled stay for the most part? This is a somewhat redundant metric designed to evaluate a second part of the hypothesis. “Net conversion” seems to track the second part better because it covers a wider range of events.

 

Metrics not chosen as either invariant of evaluation:

Number of user-ids: This metric cannot be used as an invariant because it tracks activity that would be different between experiment and control groups. In the experiment group enrollment happens after the answer on a time commitment question. For the usage as an evaluation metric it is less informative than chosen evaluation metrics because it missing the connection to a user who started free trial. We are aiming at figuring out if a user who started free trial continued with the course and enrolled which is much better represented by “Gross conversion”.

Click-through-probability: This metric cannot be an evaluation metric because it tracks activity before there is a difference between experiment and control. At the same time it does not provide any information in addition to a “Number of cookies” metric and adds complexity in calculations, so it does not make sense to use it as an invariant metric.

 

 

Measuring Standard Deviation

 

Analytic estimate of standard deviation:

Gross conversion: 0.0202

Retention: 0.0549

Net conversion: 0.0156

 

Analytic estimate for each metric would be comparable to the empirical variability due to both units of analysis and units of diversion being the same (cookies).

 

Sizing

Number of Samples vs. Power

 

No Bonferroni correction. Number of pageviews: 4741212. Sample size calculated using on-line calculator for Retention (53%, biggest probability). After that samplesize was multiplied by 2 for control and experiment and scaled by enrollement/(unique cookies to view page).

 

Duration vs. Exposure

 

I decided to divert all traffic to this experiment (fraction = 1). It would take 119 days to complete experiment if all three evaluation metrics are used, which is too long. To reduce the duration the “Retention” metric was dropped as the one taking longest time. Now only two metrics are left: Gross conversion and Net conversion. Net conversion requires largest sample size of the two: 27413 and the number of pageviews is 685325. It would take 18 days to run the experiment.

It does not seem to be risky to divert that much traffic. Participants will not be hurt because their choices will not be limited in any way in the experiment group. They will still be presented with the same choices in their course participation, but in slightly different arrangement that may actually benefit them in reducing time wasted and frustration. The sensitivity of the data is not increased in an experiment group; no additional sensitive data is collected.

Experiment Analysis

Sanity Check

 

Number of cookies (Pageviews):

Lower bound: 0.4988, upper bound: 0.5012, observed: 0.5006. Passed.

Number of clicks:

Lower bound: 0.4959, upper bound: 0.5041, observed: 0.5005. Passed.

 

Result Analysis

Effect Size Tests

 

Gross conversion:

Lower bound: -0.0291, upper bound:  -0.0120. Does not include 0, so statistically significant and does not include practical significance boundary 0.01, so is practically significant.

Net conversion:

Lower bound: -0.0116, upper bound:  0.0019. Includes 0, so statistically insignificant.

 

Sign Tests

 

Sign test results p-values:

Gross conversion: 0.0026. Statistically significant.

Net conversion: 0.6776. Not statistically significant.

 

Summary

 

I did not use Bonferroni correction. We would use Bonferroni if any one metric in a series being statistically significant would warrant launching. In this case we would want to avoid Type I error. In our case we use two metrics which account for two different parts of our hypothesis. We want both of them to be statistically significant in order to recommend launch.

 

Recommendation

 

I would not recommend launching this experiment. I believe the stated goal “reducing the number of frustrated students who left the free trial because they didn't have enough time” is achieved due to a statistical and practical significance of the gross conversion metric.

 

Net conversion metric is not significantly different between experiment and control. It is probably lower than in the original site design due to the zero locating closer to the right side of the confidence interval and probability of success in sign test 0.4348, less than 0.5. If to take in account the minimal difference established for this metric from the business prospective (dmin=0.0075), then the confidence interval includes the negative value of the practical boundary (-0.0075). It may mean that the metric decreased in value enough to not recommend a launch because it does not support the second part of the hypothesis “…without significantly reducing the number of students to continue past the free trial and eventually complete the course”.

Follow-Up Experiment

Give a high-level description of the follow up experiment you would run, what your hypothesis would be, what metrics you would want to measure, what your unit of diversion would be, and your reasoning for these choices.

 

If to reduce the number of frustrated students who cancel early in the course, I would follow-up with increasing cutoff time spent each week on the project. Five hours a week may not be a realistic time for an average person to complete the whole course in a reasonable amount of time, say few months. It is not just an amount of time per week, but also an overall rate of the progress is important for the retention. 5 hours would probably mean rather slow progression for the majority of people taking the course. I believe a cutoff time of at least 10 hours should be tested.

The metrics and the unit of diversion should remain the same as before.
